Why "Kotappakonda"?

"Kota" (కోట)

Telugu word meaning "fort" or "protected sacred space". Represents the hill's spiritual significance.

"Paka" (పాక)

Means "grove" or "flourishing vegetation area". Reflects the lush greenery surrounding the hill.

"Konda" (కొండ)

Common Telugu suffix meaning "hill" or "mountain". The three peaks represent Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva.

Historical Timeline

Ancient Period (Pre-5th CE)

Early tribal and Shaivite worship in the hill groves. Oral traditions indicate sacred water springs and caves.

Chalukya Period (7th-9th CE)

Construction of stone pathways and basic mandapas. Temple gained regional popularity in Velanadu region.

Kakatiya Dynasty (12th-14th CE)

Stone staircases and small Shiva shrines built on lower slopes. Land grants made to Brahmin priests.

Vijayanagara Period (15th-16th CE)

Grand gopuram and flagpole added. Shivaratri became major event drawing thousands annually.

British Colonial Period

Included in Madras Gazetteers. Jatara attracted over 50,000 people. Colonial maps mark "Trikoota Hills".

Natural Treasures

Trikuta Hills

Three sacred peaks rising to 1,600 feet: Brahma Konda (Creation), Vishnu Konda (Preservation), and Rudra Konda (Destruction).

Sacred Sites

Trikoteswara Swamy Temple

Main sanctum dedicated to Lord Shiva in his three-peaked form at the hill's summit. Features Vijayanagara-era architecture.

Nandi Mandapa

Pavilion with a large Nandi (Shiva's bull) facing the main sanctum. Site of special abhishekams during festivals.

Parvati Mandapam

Shrine dedicated to Goddess Parvati with exquisite carvings. Women perform special pujas here for family welfare.

Major Festivals

Mahashivaratri Jatara

Grand festival attracting 6-8 lakh devotees. Features mass chanting, temple chariot processions, and cultural programs.

Karthika Deepotsavam

Festival of lights with thousands of lamps illuminating the hill. Special pujas performed during Karthika month.

Shravana Rudrabhishekam

Special abhishekams performed during Shravana month. Devotees climb the hill with holy water from sacred rivers.

Local Economy

Temple Tourism

Devotees buy brass lamps, rudraksha malas, hill turmeric, and coconut garlands. Supports local artisans and shops.

Agriculture

Mango and tamarind orchards thrive on hill slopes. Cotton, chillies, and turmeric are major cash crops.

Handicrafts

Local artisans make clay Shiva idols, wooden walking sticks, and herbal hair oils from hill plants.

Little-Known Facts

Three-Peaked Architecture

The temple's three-peaked design is one of the few of its kind in India, representing the Trimurti.

Perennial Springs

Water springs at the hilltop never dry up - considered sacred teerthams with healing properties.

Koti Deepotsavam

Devotees light one crore lamps during special festivals for family wishes and prosperity.

Vijayanagara Shivling

The temple once housed a metal Shivling from the Vijayanagara era, now preserved in the temple vault.

Pilgrimage Routes

Some devotees climb the hill on their knees or with special vows as acts of devotion.

Tribal Connections

Early tribal communities considered the hill a spiritual zone before temple construction.
