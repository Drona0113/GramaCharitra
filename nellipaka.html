Why "Nellipaka"?

"Nelli" (నెల్లి)

Telugu word for Indian Gooseberry (Amla), a sacred medicinal fruit revered in Ayurveda and tribal medicine.

"Paka" (పాక)

Means "grove", "zone", or "spreading area of flora". Found in ancient settlements with botanical significance.

Sacred Interpretation

"Nellipaka" translates to "The Amla Grove" or "Region of Gooseberries", reflecting its historic forest cover of sacred Amla trees.

Historical Timeline

Prehistoric Times

Part of the ancient forest belt of Dandakaranya. Rock shelters and cave engravings found nearby.

Ramayana Era

Believed to have been crossed by Rama and Sita during exile. Tribal communities worship trees associated with Sita.

Kakatiya Rule (12th-14th CE)

Area used for timber and medicinal collection, guarded by Koya and Konda Reddy tribal clans.

British Colonial Period

Came under Agency Tracts; forest guesthouses built for British officials during inspections.

Post-Independence

Hub of tribal land movements in 1980s-90s. Now peaceful with eco-focused development initiatives.

Natural Treasures

Godavari River

The village lies along the sacred Godavari's edge, with lush riverbanks used for rituals and farming.

Amla Groves

Once covered with dense Amla trees, these sacred groves are central to tribal medicine and rituals.

Forest Wildlife

Home to peacocks, wild boars, monkeys, and revered snakes worshipped during Nagula Chavithi.

Sacred Sites

Sri Seetha Ramachandra Swamy

Located by the Godavari riverbank, built where Sita rested under an Amla tree. Major celebrations during Sri Rama Navami.

Nagula Banda

Natural serpent-shaped rock worshipped with turmeric and milk. Women pray here for childbirth and protection.

Tribal Grove Altars

Forest shrines marked by stone circles and bamboo poles, worshipped by Koya and Konda Reddy tribes.

Tribal Festivals

Amla Jatara

Celebration of the sacred Nelli (Amla) tree with offerings, dances, and herbal medicine demonstrations.

Nagula Chavithi

Snake worship festival with turmeric snake art, rangoli, and milk offerings at Nagula Banda.

Koya Harvest Festival

Tribal drumming, storytelling, seed rituals, and bamboo craft exhibitions celebrating the harvest.

Village Economy

Banana Farming

Special Nellipaka banana variety (under GI tag application) exported to Hyderabad and Vijayawada.

Turmeric Cultivation

Grown on forest fringes using traditional methods. Used in rituals and local medicine.

Forest Economy

Collection of wild honey, neem fruits, tulasi, and bamboo for crafts and construction.

Little-Known Facts

British-Era Guesthouse

A colonial forest guesthouse still stands, now used as a government outpost.

GI-Tag Bananas

Nellipaka bananas are under GI tag application due to unique taste and shelf life.

Sita Vanam

A forest path where villagers claim ancient trees "talk at night" with rustling sounds.

Tribal Resistance

Strategic location in 1980s tribal land movements against exploitation.

Sacred Snakes

Snakes are never harmed; worshipped as guardians during Nagula Chavithi.

Herbal Wisdom

Elder women pass down herbal healing chants using Amla, turmeric, and wild tulasi.
